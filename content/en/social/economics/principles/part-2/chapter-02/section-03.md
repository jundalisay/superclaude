**Fixed capital** is capital which, **without changing hands or being consumed immediately**, improves productivity.


We classify this into physical and metaphysical capital:

_ | Physical | Metaphysical
--- | --- | ---
Material | Equipment, Land, Location | None
Personal | Voice, Looks, Height, Strength | Honesty, Family Name, Reputation, Intelligence
Group | none (already in personal) | Culture, History

<!-- ## 💰 Fixed vs. Circulating Capital: The Key Difference

| Type | What It Is | What It Does | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

**Smith's analogy:** A tailor's needle is fixed capital. The cloth that goes through the needle is circulating capital. The needle lasts for thousands of garments; the cloth is consumed in one.

## 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Why This Matters: The Wealth of a Nation

Smith argued that a nation's ability to increase its wealth depends on **accumulating fixed capital** faster than its population grows.

| If a nation... | Then... |
| :--- | :--- |
| Invests in machines, tools, and skills (fixed capital). | Each worker can produce more. Wages rise. Prices fall. The nation becomes wealthier. |
| Only consumes and trades (circulating capital). | Productivity stagnates. Wealth does not grow. The nation remains poor. |

**The famous example:** Ten workers with needles (fixed capital) can produce ten thousand pins in a day. Ten workers without needles (only their hands) cannot produce a single pin. The needle—the fixed capital—is the difference between poverty and wealth.

---

## 🔁 The Maintenance Constraint

Fixed capital is not permanent. It requires **constant maintenance** and eventual replacement.

| Smith's Warning | Modern Analogy |
| :--- | :--- |
| A machine that is not repaired will break. | A factory that defers maintenance will eventually stop producing. |
| A worker who does not practice their skill will lose it. | A programmer who does not learn new languages becomes obsolete. |
| Land that is not drained or fertilized will revert to wilderness. | A farm that does not invest in soil health will lose fertility. |

**Thus, a portion of the annual product must be set aside *not* for consumption, but for the "repair and replacement" of fixed capital.** This is the origin of the economic concept of *depreciation*.

---

## 🧠 Common Misconception: Fixed Capital is Not "Money"

Smith was explicit: **Money is not fixed capital.** Money is circulating capital—it passes from hand to hand and is not a productive instrument in itself.

| If you own... | That is... | Why? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| A $100 bill | **Circulating capital** | It does not produce anything by itself. You must exchange it for tools, machines, or labor to become productive. |
| A lathe (purchased with $100) | **Fixed capital** | It directly aids in producing goods (e.g., turned table legs, metal shafts). |

**The error of mercantilism** (and of some modern politicians) is to confuse the accumulation of money (circulating capital) with the accumulation of real productive power (fixed capital).

## ✅ Conclusion

| Smith's Concept | In a Nutshell |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Fixed Capital** | Machines, buildings, improved land, and human skills. |
| **What it does** | Increases the productivity of labor without being consumed. |
| **Examples** | A needle, a warehouse, a drained field, a trained carpenter. |
| **Not fixed capital** | Money, raw materials, wages (these are circulating capital). |
| **Key requirement** | Maintenance and replacement, or it will decay. |

**In short: Fixed capital is the *stock* of productive power that a society has built up over time. It is the difference between a hunter-gatherer band and an industrial civilization. And it is only useful if it is maintained, not just accumulated.** -->
