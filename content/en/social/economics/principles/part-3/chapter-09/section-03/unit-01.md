The Mechanism: Private Contraction and Public Expansion
The core economic shock can be broken down into three phases:

1. The Private Sector Shock (Contraction) 📉
The pandemic triggered an unprecedented reduction in global economic activity, initially causing massive private sector contraction:

Supply Chain & Retail Freeze: Global demand plummeted, leading to supply chain disruptions and a collapse in private retail activity, freezing capital flow.

Venture Capital Retreat: While some tech saw growth (Zoom, remote work), speculative investment was broadly hit. The Silicon Valley banking crisis (which includes the collapse of Signature and Silvergate alongside Silicon Valley Bank) is a later, symptomatic example of this volatility. These banks, which financed highly leveraged private ventures, were vulnerable because the easy money environment of the pandemic's aftermath ended abruptly, exposing systemic weaknesses in the private tech funding ecosystem.

Job Loss and Wage Stagnation: Mass layoffs and reduced hours severely limited the individual private income stream, particularly in low-wage service economies (e.g., Bangladesh's garment sector, Nepal's tourism).

2. The Public Sector Overcorrection (Expansion) ⬆️
To prevent total economic collapse, governments worldwide injected trillions of dollars into their economies, shifting control of money flow:

Quantitative Easing and Stimulus: Nations like the US provided direct payments and dramatically lowered interest rates, effectively flooding the financial system with public-controlled cash.

Industrial Policy and Tariffs: The shift toward protectionism, exemplified by policies like the Trump tariffs (and their persistence/modification under subsequent administrations), represents a deliberate re-routing of money. Tariffs and subsidies move money away from global supply chains (private sector efficiency) and redirect it to domestic, often politically favored, public or quasi-public domestic industries.

Local Infrastructure & Debt: Developing nations (like Indonesia and Madagascar) took on significant sovereign debt to cover social safety nets and infrastructure, concentrating national wealth and future revenues under government control.

3. The Unrest Phase: The Fight for the Re-routed Cash ⚔️
The imbalance creates the conditions for protest because a vast segment of the population feels alienated from the wealth concentrated in the public sphere, leading to:

Political/Cultural Wars as different groups fight for legislative control over the vastly expanded public budget and policy direction (e.g., funding for specific industries, trade policy).

Conclusion
The pandemic didn't just cause a recession; it caused a tectonic shift in financial control. The current wave of protests isn't just about economic hardship; it's about the distribution of the wealth and power that was abruptly nationalized or politicized during the crisis. The resulting instability will likely continue until the global economy either re-establishes a stable private-to-public ratio or creates new political structures capable of managing the massive, polarized public sector purse.
