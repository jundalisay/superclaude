The pH scale (*potential of hydrogen*) tells how acidic or basic a solution is.

The pH scale transforms an invisible sea of ions into a simple number. It measures the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺) — specifically hydronium (H₃O⁺) — in water. 

The scale runs from **0 to 14** and is logarithmic, meaning each whole number represents a tenfold change in acidity.

- **Low pH (0–6): Acidic** → More H⁺ ions.
- **pH 7: Neutral** → Pure water.
- **High pH (8–14): Basic (alkaline)** → Fewer H⁺ ions, more OH⁻ ions.


## The Formula (Simplified)

**pH = –log₁₀[H⁺]**  

Because of the negative log, a high H⁺ concentration gives a low pH. For example:  
- [H⁺] = 0.1 M (10⁻¹) → pH = 1 (very acidic)  
- [H⁺] = 0.0000001 M (10⁻⁷) → pH = 7 (neutral)

Thus, a solution at pH 3 is **10 times more acidic** than one at pH 4, and 100 times more than pH 5.

## How Do We Measure pH?

- **Litmus paper:** Turns red in acid, blue in base. Quick but imprecise.
- **pH indicator solutions:** Liquids that change color across specific ranges (e.g., phenolphthalein turns pink above pH 8).
- **pH meter:** An electronic probe that gives a precise digital reading — standard in labs.

## Why Does pH Matter?

- **Biology:** Human blood stays tightly between pH 7.35–7.45. A shift of 0.2 can be dangerous. Stomach acid (pH ~1.5) aids digestion.
- **Agriculture:** Most crops prefer slightly acidic soil (pH 6–7). Too low? Lime (a base) raises it. Too high? Sulfur lowers it.
- **Industry:** Swimming pools need pH 7.2–7.8 to prevent eye irritation and protect equipment. Wastewater treatment adjusts pH to precipitate heavy metals.
- **Oceans:** Rising CO₂ makes seawater more acidic (ocean acidification), threatening coral and shellfish.
