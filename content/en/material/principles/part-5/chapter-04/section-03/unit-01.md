### High Density

These have the lowest mass as they are closest to space. Rather than call them up and down quarks, we call them the **forward-wristwatch** and **reverse-wristwatch**.

### Medium Density
These are heavier, unstable copies. Rather than **charm quark** and **strange quark**, which are excited versions of the up and down, we call them the **forward-wallclock** and **reverse-wallclock**.

Like the slower mainspring wheel of a wall clock, these spin at a lower frequency and carry significantly more mass. They appear briefly in high-energy collisions, such as those in particle accelerators or cosmic rays. 

A wallclock micro-aetherspace quickly sheds its excess energy and decays back down into the lighter wristwatch variety, which is why we never find them in ordinary stable matter.


### Low Density

The most massive micro-aetherspaces we call the **forward-sundial** and **reverse-sundial**, rather than the top quark and bottom quark.

Like the imperceptibly slow turning of a sundial's shadow, these spin at the lowest frequency of all — and Nature pays a steep price in mass for that near-stillness. 

The forward-sundial is a true titan, as heavy as a tungsten atom, and exists for only a minuscule fraction of a second before decaying. 

The reverse-sundial is lighter but still far heavier than a proton. Both appear only in the most violent high-energy events in the universe, and both cascade rapidly down through the wallclock and wristwatch stages as they shed their enormous aetheric pressure back toward equilibrium.


The three tiers — **wristwatch, wallclock, sundial** — thus form a natural hierarchy of spin frequency to mass:
- the faster the spin, the lighter and more stable the micro-aetherspace
- the slower the spin, the heavier and more fleeting it becomes
