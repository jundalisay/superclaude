{{< l a="Foreigner" >}}
Instruction is the remedy to ignorance.

There are many kinds of the art of instruction. At any rate there are two principal ones. 

<!-- I believe that I can see how we shall soonest arrive at the answer to this question. -->

A division of ignorance into 2 parts will imply that the art of instruction is also twofold, answering to the two divisions of ignorance.

I do seem to myself to see one very large and bad sort of ignorance which is quite separate, and may be weighed in the scale against all other sorts of ignorance put together.

The great source of all the errors of the intellect is when a person supposes that he knows, but does not know.

This is the kind of ignorance which is called stupidity.

What should we call the sort of instruction which gets rid of this?
{{< /l >}}


{{< r a="Theaetetus" >}}
That instruction is called education. It is not the teaching of handicraft arts.
{{< /r >}}


{{< l a="Foreigner" >}}
Yes, Theaetetus, and by nearly all Hellenes. But is education further divisible?

<!-- I think that there is a point at which such a division is possible. -->

Of education, one method appears to be rougher, and another smoother.

There is the time-honoured mode which our fathers commonly practised towards their sons and is still adopted by many.
- They either roughly reprove their errors, or
- Gently advise them

Both are called admonition.

But some people think that:
- all ignorance is involuntary
- no wise man is willing to learn things which he is clever in 
- admonitory instruction gives much trouble and does little good

Accordingly, they try to eradicate the spirit of conceit in another way.

They:
- cross-examine a man's words when he thinks that he is saying something and is really saying nothing.
- easily convict him of inconsistencies in his opinions

These they then collect by the dialectical process.

They place them side by side, show that they contradict one another about the same things, in relation to the same things.

He, seeing this, is angry with himself, and grows gentle towards others.

Thus, he is entirely delivered from great prejudices and harsh notions, in a way which is most amusing to the hearer, and produces the most lasting good effect on the person who is the subject of the operation. 

The physician considers that the body will receive no benefit from taking food until the internal obstacles have been removed.

Likewise, the purifier of the soul knows that his patient will receive no benefit from the application of knowledge until he is refuted, and from refutation learns modesty.

He must be purged of his prejudices first and made to think that he knows only what he knows, and no more.

This is why refutation is the greatest and chiefest of purifications.

He who has not been refuted, though he be the Great King himself, is in an awful state of impurity.

He is uninstructed and deformed in those things in which he who would be truly blessed should be fairest and purest.

The Sophists are the ministers of this art.

<!-- Lest we should assign to them too high a prerogative. -->
{{< /l >}}


{{< r a="Theaetetus" >}}
The Sophist has a certain likeness to our minister of purification.
{{< /r >}}

{{< l a="Foreigner" >}}
Yes, it is like the similarity between a wolf, the fiercest of animals, to a dog, the gentlest. 

But he who would not be found tripping, ought to be very careful in this matter of comparisons, for they are most slippery things. Nevertheless, let us assume that the Sophists are the men. 

I say this provisionally, for I think that the line which divides them will be marked enough if proper care is taken.

From the discerning art comes purification. From purification let there be separated off a part which is concerned with the soul.

Of this mental purification, instruction is a portion, and of instruction education, and of education, that refutation of vain conceit which has been discovered in the present argument.

I call this the nobly-descended art of Sophistry.
{{< /l >}}


THEAETETUS: Very well. Yet, the Sophist presents himself in a number of forms. This makes me doubt how I can describe his real nature confidently.


{{< l a="Foreigner" >}}
<!-- You naturally feel perplexed; and yet I think that he must be still more perplexed in his attempt to escape us, for as the proverb says, when every way is blocked, there is no escape; now, then, is the time of all others to set upon him.

First let us wait a moment and recover breath, and while we are resting, we may reckon up in how many forms he has appeared.  -->

In the first place, he was discovered to be a paid hunter after wealth and youth.

In the second place, he was a merchant in the goods of the soul.

In the third place, he has turned out to be a retailer of the same sort of wares.
{{< /l >}}


THEAETETUS: Yes; and in the fourth place, he himself manufactured the learned wares which he sold.



{{< l a="Foreigner" >}}
Quite right. 

The fifth is the fighting class. It was further distinguished as a hero of debate, who professed the eristic art.

The sixth point was doubtful, and yet we at last agreed that he was a purger of souls, who cleared away notions obstructive to knowledge.


Do you not see that when the professor of any art has one name and many kinds of knowledge, there must be something wrong? 

The multiplicity of names applied to him shows that the common principle to which all these branches of knowledge are tending, is not understood.

<!-- At any rate we will understand him, and no indolence shall prevent us. Let us begin again, then, and re-examine some of our statements concerning the Sophist; there was one thing which appeared to me especially characteristic of him. -->

We said that he was a disputer who also teaches others the art of disputation.

About what does he profess that he teaches men to dispute? To begin at the beginning—Does he make them able to dispute about divine things, which are invisible to men in general?

What do you say of the visible things in heaven and earth, and the like?
{{< /l >}}


THEAETETUS: Certainly he disputes, and teaches to dispute about them.
