Section 2 reduced the whole theory of Dynamics into a formula. 

It needs only to be developed to give all the equations necessary for the solution of any problem of this Dynamics.

But this development, which is only a matter of pure calculation, can still be simplified in several respects by the means we will employ in this Section.

As everything consists in reducing the different variables which enter into the formula in question to the smallest possible number, by means of the equations of condition given by the nature of each problem, one of the principal operations is to substitute, in place of these variables, functions of other variables. This object is always easy to fulfill by ordinary methods; but there is a particular manner of satisfying it relative to the proposed formula, which has the advantage of always leading directly to the simplest transformed expression.

This formula is composed of two different parts which must be considered separately.

The first contains the terms


...


which come uniquely from the forces resulting from the inertia of bodies.

The second is composed of the terms

...

due to the accelerating forces \( \mathrm{P,\,Q,\,R},\ldots \) which are supposed to act effectively on each body along the lines \( p,q,r,\ldots \) and which tend to diminish these lines. 

The sum of these two quantities, being set equal to zero, constitutes the general formula of Dynamics (sect. II, art. 5).
