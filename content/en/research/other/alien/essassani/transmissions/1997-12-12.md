## **Conversation on Vanity and Flattery**

Questioner: I have a question about vanity, flattery. I was listening to a tape of Barbara Marciniak who talked about the Pleiadians — it could have been Barbara, I don't think it was a Pleiadian — but she said something about don't ever fall for flattery. 

What came to mind was that story you told us about happy fox and sad fox. I never really got any moral out of that. Has that got something to do with it?

Bashar: That's exactly the moral. The idea that happy fox was just being who he was.

The idea that sad fox, in attempting to get happy fox to aggrandize who he was for being happy, to make himself something more than he was. And then when happy fox bought into that flattery, that is when happy fox died and was no longer in existence as the true happy fox, as himself.

Therefore, it is that when you accept information about yourself — not that you cannot recognize that you are beautiful, special, wondrous, and unique — but when in your terms you allow it to go to your head, when it becomes the sole purpose for the communication to simply embellish upon yourself those ideas of name and place, and that's all you become, then the real you in a sense has died, has vanished, and you're really not there anymore. You're nothing but a shell of your former self.
